Part 1: Masses and springs

In this part, the foundation of a cloth is created, using point masses and springs that connect those point masses. To more accurately model the cloth, three constraints are considered. First, structural constraints attach springs between horizontally and vertically directly adjacent masses. Second, shearing constraints attach springs between diagonal masses. Finally, bending constraints attach springs between masses that are two masses away, horizontally and vertically.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

In this part, the cloth simulation was implemented using Verlet integration to compute mass positions after time steps dt. Various parameters including the density of the cloth, the spring constant between the point masses, and the damping value.

The spring constant, ks, and the density of the cloth are closely related. As the spring constant increases, the springs become tighter, resulting in a more taut and stiff cloth. A lower spring constant means looser connections between the masses, resulting in a more saggy cloth. Conversely, a lower density results in less mass, and therefore less acceleration due to gravity. A less dense cloth is lighter and does not sag, while a more dense cloth is heavier, experiencing more acceleration due to gravity, and crinkles more.

The damping value causes the point masses to move slower at each time step, simulating energy lost in the cloth due to non-conservative forces such as friction. A larger damping value corresponds to greater loss in energy, resulting in slower movement in the cloth, while a smaller value corresponds to a frictionless environment.

Below are images of the equilibrated cloth with different ks, density, and damping values. Note the damping value only impacts the speed of movement, but not the final state. Additionally, increased spring constant increases stiffness, while increased density results in sagging, a constant ratio between the two results in a visually similar cloth.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

In this part, point mass collisions with sphere and plane objects were implemented, resulting in interaction between the cloth and the objects. The collision implementation involved determining whether each point mass collided with each object using its current and most recent positions. If a point mass is determined to intersect, the point of intersection is calculated and used to determine a new decreased change in distance for each time step. This prevents the cloth from ever fully reaching the object's surface and also accounts for friction between the cloth and the object.

Below are images of the cloth at rest on a plane and the sphere simulation with different spring constants. As the spring constant increases, the connections between each point mass become more tense, resulting in a less drapable and more sharply folded cloth.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

Prior to this part, the grid of point masses was only constrained by each local mass's springs. This part implements self-collisions between point masses using spatial hashing. Rather than checking each point's relation to every other point mass, a hash table is used to determine local point masses to check while ignoring the rest.

To do this, the volume containing the cloth is partitioned into 3D boxes and every point mass is mapped to one of these boxes. Iterating through only the local box each point mass is contained in is effective and far more efficient than iterating through every point mass.
Adjusting the density and spring constant of the cloth also changes how it interacts with itself. Similar to interactions with objects, the ratio of density to spring constant is a reasonable predictor of similarity. With a higher ratio (increased density), the cloth prefers flatter orientations more similar to a piece of paper. With a decreased ratio (increased spring constant), the cloth easily folds in on itself. With a similar ratio (both increased spring constant and density), the cloth exhibits similar behavior.
